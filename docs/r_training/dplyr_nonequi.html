Introduction

simple data sets:

library(dplyr)

a <- tibble(id = 1:5, 
       score_a = sample(1:10, 5, T))

b <- tibble(id = 1:5, 
       score_b = sample(1:10, 5, T))

Standard joins relate rows from datasets by identity. For example, we can join by shared values in the id column:

a |>
  inner_join(b, by = join_by(id))
# A tibble: 5 Ã— 3
     id score_a score_b
  <int>   <int>   <int>
1     1       8       4
2     2       7       8
3     3       6       6
4     4      10       7
5     5       1       3

We could convert this into a non-equi join by adding an inequality:

a |>
  left_join(b, by = join_by(id, score_a > score_b))
# A tibble: 5 Ã— 3
     id score_a score_b
  <int>   <int>   <int>
1     1       8       4
2     2       7      NA
3     3       6      NA
4     4      10       7
5     5       1      NA

Here, weâ€™ve added a condition that restricts the join to cases where score_a is larger than score_b. Thatâ€™s all a non-equi join is: a join based on a non-equality. Usually, weâ€™d use this with an inner join that will drop the NAs for us:

a |>
  inner_join(b, by = join_by(id, score_a > score_b))
# A tibble: 2 Ã— 3
     id score_a score_b
  <int>   <int>   <int>
1     1       8       4
2     4      10       7

Effectively this is equivalent to a filter then a join:

a |>
  left_join(b, by = join_by(id)) |>
  filter(score_a > score_b)
# A tibble: 2 Ã— 3
     id score_a score_b
  <int>   <int>   <int>
1     1       8       4
2     4      10       7

But faster and more elegant, especially if youâ€™re matching a small range of values in the non-equi part of the join.
